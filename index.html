Decade Summary

1940's

Electromechanical computers developed using electrical switches to move mechanical parts.


Code-breaking became crucial during WWII:
Top-secret operations at Bletchley Park led by Alan Turing.
Enigma machine and Bombe used electromechanical technology for code-breaking.
These were not general-purpose computers but advanced technology for their time.
The intelligence gathered from Bletchley Park shortened WWII by two years, saving an estimated 14 million lives.

1950's

Transistors revolutionized computing hardware, replacing vacuum tubes with smaller, faster, and more reliable alternatives.

1960's

The third generation of computers introduced integrated circuits

Apollo Guidance Computer (AGC) designed at MIT: pivotal for the Apollo moon landing missions. Margaret Hamilton led the software development team.

Minicomputers became portable and practical during this era.

1970's

Microprocessors transformed computing, enabling personal computers and introducing compact, affordable designs.

1980's

The 1980s saw the rise of personal computers (IBM PC, Apple Macintosh), GUIs, Microsoft Windows, and the CD-ROM. Networking foundations like DNS and the early web emerged, alongside gaming milestones like the NES.

1990's

The 1990s were a transformative decade, marked by the rise of the internet, advancements in software and hardware, and the emergence of technologies that continue to shape our world today.

2000's

The 2000s were a decade of rapid technological growth, laying the foundation for the digital age we live in today.

2010's

The 2010s were a decade of innovation, with advancements in AI, smart devices, and connectivity shaping the modern digital landscape.

2020's

The 2020s are shaping up as the Decade of AI and Sustainability, with significant advancements in artificial intelligence, biotechnology, energy, and space exploration leading the way.

Future

The future holds immense possibilities with AI, quantum computing, and sustainable technologies shaping the next era.

Grace Hopper

Grace Hopper documented the "first actual case of a bug being found".

Grace Hopper was also a pioneer in programming languages, developing the first compiler in 1952. Her A-0 System translated human-readable instructions into machine code, laying the foundation for modern programming languages like COBOL. Her work made software development more accessible and significantly advanced computing.

Bugs and Debugging

The term "bug" in engineering and computers refers to faults or errors in a mechanism or program.

The first recorded use of the term for this purpose was in 1878 when Thomas Edison referenced the term in a letter.

Another term common in computing today is "debugging", the act of finding the root of a problem in a program which inhibits it from working correctly, and removing or correcting the error, i.e the process of removing a 'bug'.

One of the first references of "debugging" was made by Robert Oppenheimer in 1944, in a letter to Ernest Lawrence.

However, it is quite likely that neither of these terms would be used as they currently are now in computing if it weren't for a team of engineers working on the Harvard Mark II Computer. The computer was not operating correctly due to a moth, that the team later discovered caught in one of the relays of the machine

One of the members of this team, Grace Hopper, documented this event in a journal on 9th September 1947. She taped the moth to the page and described it as the "first actual case of a bug being found".
